The Oldest Building in Detroit

A House That
Built a City

In 1826, a young entrepreneur from Albany, New York, built the finest frame house in the Michigan Territory — far from the city walls, on open farmland along the River Road. That man was Charles Christopher Trowbridge, and his home at 1380 East Jefferson Avenue has endured for two centuries as the oldest documented building in Detroit.


It has survived the cholera epidemics, the Great Fire of 1805's aftermath, the rise and fall of industries, the automobile revolution, and Detroit's dramatic reinvention. Through it all, the Trowbridge House has stood — a testament to the enduring spirit of the entrepreneurs who built Detroit and continue to build it today.

At the time of its construction, the Trowbridge House was considered the finest frame house in the Michigan Territory.
The Man Who Built It

Charles Christopher Trowbridge

Born December 29, 1800 in Albany, New York, Charles Trowbridge arrived in Detroit as a nineteen-year-old with nothing but ambition and a sharp mind. He would go on to become one of Detroit's most important early citizens — serving as U.S. Deputy Marshal, explorer alongside Lewis Cass, interpreter and ethnographer of Native American cultures, president of the Bank of Michigan, railroad executive, alderman, mayor during the cholera epidemic of 1834, Whig candidate for governor, and a regent of the University of Michigan.


In 1826, the same year he married Catherine Whipple Sibley — daughter of Detroit's first appointed mayor, Solomon Sibley — Trowbridge built this house on what was then farmland far from the city center. He chose this location because of his strong rapport with the local Native American tribes. He lived here for 57 years until his death on April 3, 1883. His wife preceded him, passing in 1880. Both are buried in Detroit's Elmwood Cemetery.

Four Decades of Care

The Stewardship of Stanley B. Dickson, Jr.

When Stanley B. Dickson Jr. acquired the Trowbridge House in 1981, decades before Detroit's modern renaissance had begun, he saw what Charles Trowbridge himself saw nearly two centuries earlier: a place with enormous potential, worth investing in when others might have walked away.


Dickson transformed the house into a thriving professional office while maintaining deep respect for its historical integrity. Historians from the Detroit Historical Society advised on period-accurate color choices. The house's exterior features have been carefully maintained. The original ambiance — the natural light, the central hallway connecting front and rear entrances, the sense of a home rather than a sterile office — has been preserved.


In 1993, Dickson expanded by acquiring the neighboring Bosquett Building, connecting the two structures while preserving the Trowbridge House's character. Under his watch, the campus has housed not one or two but six operating businesses — a modern-day echo of Trowbridge's own restless, multi-enterprise spirit.


Owning a 200-year-old structure is not without its challenges. Underground water pipes, aged infrastructure, the constant vigilance required to preserve a building that predates every other standing structure in the city. But Dickson has never wavered. His stewardship has ensured that Detroit's oldest building isn't just preserved — it's alive, productive, and relevant.

An Unbroken Thread

An Entrepreneur's House.
Then and Now.

Charles C. Trowbridge was, at his core, an entrepreneur. A banker. A railroad executive. A land developer. A man who saw opportunity on open farmland and built something that would last. Two hundred years later, the same spirit animates the Trowbridge House. Stanley Dickson has carried that torch for over four decades — building businesses, creating jobs, and preserving history. And the entrepreneurs who work within these walls today continue the legacy, building companies that serve communities across the nation.


The address is the same. The ambition is the same. The house endures.
