The Oldest Building in Detroit

A House That
Built a City

In 1826, a young entrepreneur from Albany, New York, built the finest frame house in the Michigan Territory — far from the city walls, on open farmland along the River Road. That man was Charles Christopher Trowbridge, and his home at 1380 East Jefferson Avenue has endured for two centuries as the oldest documented building in Detroit.


It has survived the cholera epidemics, the Great Fire of 1805's aftermath, the rise and fall of industries, the automobile revolution, and Detroit's dramatic reinvention. Through it all, the Trowbridge House has stood — a testament to the enduring spirit of the entrepreneurs who built Detroit and continue to build it today.

At the time of its construction, the Trowbridge House was considered the finest frame house in the Michigan Territory.
The Man Who Built It

Charles Christopher Trowbridge

Born December 29, 1800 in Albany, New York, Charles Trowbridge arrived in Detroit as a nineteen-year-old with nothing but ambition and a sharp mind. He would go on to become one of Detroit's most important early citizens — serving as U.S. Deputy Marshal, explorer alongside Lewis Cass, interpreter and ethnographer of Native American cultures, president of the Bank of Michigan, railroad executive, alderman, mayor during the cholera epidemic of 1834, Whig candidate for governor, and a regent of the University of Michigan.


In 1826, the same year he married Catherine Whipple Sibley — daughter of Detroit's first appointed mayor, Solomon Sibley — Trowbridge built this house on what was then farmland far from the city center. He chose this location because of his strong rapport with the local Native American tribes. He lived here for 57 years until his death on April 3, 1883. His wife preceded him, passing in 1880. Both are buried in Detroit's Elmwood Cemetery.

A Family's Stewardship

The Dickson Family & the Trowbridge House

Charles Trowbridge built this house as a family home — and for the past four decades, it has been one again. Three generations of the Dickson family have invested in, preserved, and built businesses from within these walls.

Stanley B. Dickson, Jr.

Founder · Attorney · CPA · Entrepreneur

In 1981 — years before Detroit's modern renaissance had a heartbeat — Stanley Dickson acquired the Trowbridge House and saw what Charles Trowbridge himself had seen nearly two centuries earlier: a place with enormous potential, worth investing in when others might have walked away. A University of Michigan graduate with honors, Dickson earned his M.S. in Taxation from Golden Gate University and his J.D. from the University of Detroit. He is a certified public accountant, chartered life underwriter, attorney, and registered real estate broker.


Under his stewardship, historians from the Detroit Historical Society advised on period-accurate restorations. The house's exterior features have been carefully maintained. The original ambiance — natural light, the central hallway connecting front and rear entrances, the feeling of a home rather than a sterile office — has been preserved. In 1993, he expanded by acquiring the neighboring 1899 Bosquett Building, connecting the two structures while preserving the Trowbridge House's character.


Over four decades, Dickson has founded and led more than a dozen businesses from the Trowbridge House — from Trowbridge Law Firm and Dickson & Associates to Marketplace Homes, Genesis Genetics Institute, Windsor Hotel Development, the Epicurean Group, Happy Howie's, Trowbridge Capital Partners, First National Bank in Howell, and Finance Michigan. He serves on the board of Barton Malow Enterprises, served as Chapter Chair of the World Presidents' Organization, and is a trustee of Cleary University and the Stan & Heather Dickson Charitable Trust.

The Next Generation

A Multigenerational House

Charles Trowbridge lived in this house for 57 years and raised his family here. His daughter Sarah married George Hendrie. The house stayed in the Trowbridge family for over a century. Today, the Dickson family carries that same tradition forward — Stanley's sons William and Daniel work alongside their father at 1380 East Jefferson, building businesses together under the same roof where it all started.

It's a family affair in the truest sense. Three Dicksons, one address, four decades of enterprise — and counting. Just as the Trowbridge name was synonymous with this house for a century, the Dickson family is writing its own multigenerational chapter in the story of Detroit's oldest building.

An Unbroken Thread

An Entrepreneur's House.
Then and Now.

Charles C. Trowbridge was, at his core, an entrepreneur. A banker. A railroad executive. A land developer. A man who saw opportunity on open farmland and built something that would last. Two hundred years later, the same spirit animates the Trowbridge House. Stanley Dickson has carried that torch for over four decades — building businesses, creating jobs, and preserving history. And the entrepreneurs who work within these walls today continue the legacy, building companies that serve communities across the nation.


The address is the same. The ambition is the same. The house endures.
